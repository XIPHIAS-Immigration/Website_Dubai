# Opportunities for Overseas Nurses Looking to Migrate to New Zealand

**Written by Guest | By Varun Singh**  
**Published on:** April 6, 2024 | 16:26 IST  

---

## Overview

In recent years, New Zealand has been grappling with a critical shortage of nurses, a situation exacerbated by an aging population and the retirement of experienced nurses. As of 2024, the country continues to face this challenge, with high-demand opportunities for overseas nurses looking to migrate. The Nursing Council of New Zealand predicts a shortfall of **15,000 nurses by 2035**, highlighting the urgency of the situation.

To address this crisis, the government is working on several initiatives, including increasing resources for training and retaining qualified nurses, providing better financial aid options for students, offering incentives for experienced nurses to stay employed, and expanding access to online education.

The government is also collaborating with immigration authorities and nursing unions to bring more nurses into the country, with over **1,200 visa applications** already submitted for work in New Zealand’s health system. Additionally, the government is encouraging more people to train as nurses and re-employing the **“latent nursing workforce”**, which includes former nurses who have lost their annual practicing certificates.

---

## The Heart of the Issue

The heart of the nursing shortage issue in New Zealand stems from various factors, including:

### Healthcare System Restructuring
Recent changes in the healthcare system, such as hospital closures and mergers, have resulted in fewer positions for nurses, contributing to the shortage.

### Financial Barriers
Rising tuition costs and limited financial aid options have led to fewer nursing students completing their studies, reducing the number of qualified candidates entering the workforce each year.

### Recruitment Challenges
Difficulty in recruiting highly qualified and experienced nurses, limited immigration options, and immigration restrictions have hindered efforts to fill nursing positions.

### Patient Impact
The shortage has directly impacted patient care and safety, leading to longer wait times for appointments and procedures, delays in diagnosis or treatment, increased medical errors, and poor patient outcomes.

To address this crisis, the New Zealand government is implementing policies aimed at increasing resources for training and retaining qualified nurses, providing better financial aid options for students, offering incentives for experienced nurses to stay employed, expanding access to online education, and introducing new immigration pathways. These measures are crucial to alleviate workforce pressures and ensure quality patient care across New Zealand’s healthcare system.

---

## Nursing Shortage in New Zealand

The New Zealand government is implementing several policies to address the nursing shortage:

- **Increasing clinical placement opportunities:**  
  The government has announced an increase in clinical placements for nursing students, allowing hundreds more home-grown nurses to gain their registration to work in New Zealand.

- **Supporting domestic training:**  
  The government is supporting an additional **700 clinical placements** nationwide for the 2024 intake, aiming to boost the future nursing workforce by about 10%.

- **Improving working conditions:**  
  Nurse pay has been increased by **14%**, with targeted support to reduce attrition among Māori and Pacific students who face higher dropout rates.

- **Encouraging international recruitment:**  
  The government has removed barriers to migration for qualified overseas nurses and midwives, making it easier for them to choose New Zealand as their next home.

- **Supporting rural and interdisciplinary training:**  
  Expansion of rural and interdisciplinary programs will create **135 new training places annually** for allied and scientific professionals.

- **Reducing reliance on global recruitment:**  
  The aim is to build a self-sufficient, diverse workforce by strengthening rural health teams and developing homegrown talent.

These initiatives form part of the **Te Whatu Ora and Te Aka Whai Ora Health Workforce Plan 2023/24**, designed to relieve immediate workforce pressures and prepare for future healthcare challenges.

---

## The Role of Immigration Consultants

Immigration consultants play a significant role in addressing the nursing shortage by facilitating the migration of **internationally qualified nurses** to fill vacancies in the healthcare sector. These consultants assist with navigating visa processes, securing work permits, and meeting health and character requirements.

The influx of internationally trained nurses — particularly from countries like **India and the Philippines** — has been crucial in bridging workforce gaps. Consultants help streamline visa applications, guide nurses through cultural and professional adaptation, and ensure a smooth relocation process.

Moreover, initiatives such as the **“Green List”** include nurses as part of New Zealand’s fast-tracked residency pathway, encouraging skilled healthcare professionals to settle long-term. The contribution of immigration consultants remains pivotal in enabling qualified nurses to integrate efficiently into New Zealand’s healthcare system.

---

## Conclusion

As New Zealand navigates through its healthcare challenges, the combined efforts of **government policies and international recruitment** are essential to addressing the nursing shortage. With the predicted shortfall of 15,000 nurses by 2035, these initiatives must continue evolving to ensure a robust and sustainable healthcare system for the future.
